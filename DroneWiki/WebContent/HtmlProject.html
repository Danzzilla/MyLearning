History and Background

  People have been flying radio controlled aircrafts for a very long time now. First it began with kites, then remote controlled planes which were connected with a wire, and then wireless remote controlled aircrafts came about, and later drones. Drones can vary from having 3-8+ rotors.

  Drones can be used for many things. Many are used for videography, others for farming, military, and even for racing. Like racing drones, some drones use an extra camera and video transmitting system to send the video signal instantaneously to a pair of video goggles that the pilots use. This technology makes flying at high speeds very possible. And by high speed I mean 100+ miles per hour, reaching 60 mph from a standstill in under a second!

Frame

  To survive a crash at 100+mph the drone needs to be very durable. To achieve this durability without sacrificing the drone weight, racing drones need a carbon fiber frame. The carbon fiber is durable enough to withstand hard crashes and light enough to keep the drone's weight at a minimum. Personally, I use Armattan and Lumenier frames.

  Some frames come with just a top plate and a single bottom plate that holds on all the motors and other electronic components. Other frames have a top and bottom plate and separate arms that bolt onto the main plate which holds most of the electronics. Some frame companies do this because some pilots break drone arms a lot who would rather buy a new arm than a whole new bottom plate and have to transfer everything from one frame to the new one. Now although these frames can break during a hard crash, they can withstand many good crashes at 60mph before the frame actually breaks. (it also depends how you crash though)

Flight Controller

  The flight controller is the brain of the drone. In the flight controller, you fine tune how you want the drone to fly, enable different features that the drone can use, how sensitive everything is, and much more. Some of the features that racing drones have is turtle mode. You might wonder what that does, does it make the drone slow? Nope. If you crash the drone and it is upside down on the ground, you can enable turtle mode and flip over the drone back to its regular orientation remotely. Cool huh.

  A handfull of flight controllers also process the on screen display that you see through the goggles. You will have things like battery voltage, signal strength, and things like that displayed on the screen you are looking at. Also thanks to that, you can tune the drone on the go without having to hook up the flight controller to a computer. Some flight controllers have a power distribution board built in to it, distributing power from the battery to every single component on the drone. If the flight controller doesnt have one, you would need to get a deducated power distribution board.

Electronic Speed Controller

  A drone has one electronic speed controller (commonly called an ESC) for every motor. An ESC recieves the signal sent from the flight controller of what that specific motor has to do and translates that information into pulses that it sends out to the electromagnets in the motor. Some ESC's send out telemetry data to the flight controller so the pilot can see how the motors are doing mid-flight.

Motor

  The motors are one of the main reasons a racing drone can fly so fast. The motors used in racing drones are brushless, meaning theres an outer part with magnets and an inner part which sends out electric pulses causing the magnets to move away.

  Motors are rated differently. Higher rpm motors are meant for lighter loads (weight or propeller pitch) and lower rpm motors are meant for heavier loads. So if you want to run a gopro and/or aggressive propellers on a drone you would want to go with lower rpm motors. If not, you would get a higher rpm motor. Racing drone pilots like to get the drone as light as possible so they can run both aggressive propellers AND high rpm motors to be able to get more speed. Higher load also means you need ESC's that can handle a higher amperage.

FPV

  FPV stands for first person view. An FPV system consists of a camera, a video transmitter, a video reciever, and a monitor that displays the recived video. An on screen display (OSD) chip is optional, it allows you to see different data coming from the drone displayed to the screen. Racing drone pilots prefer to use video goggles instead of monitors for FPV because it is more immersive and allows them to focus on the flight and fly from the cockpit of the drone.

  An FPV system first recieves video from the FPV camera, which sends the signal to the OSD if it has one, then the altered video is sent to the video transmitter which sends the signal out and the video reciever catches the signal and sends it to the monitor to display the image. All this happens instantaneously because if there was a 0.1 second delay in all that, drone pilots would have to predict where the drone actually is in current time and make maneuvers ahead of time. If you have never seen a video of a drone race click here (and yes, the video is real time and not sped up), you can see that if there was a 0.1 second delay in the video feed it would be nearly impossible to do all that.
